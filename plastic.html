Plastic

The synthetic solution

Invented 1869 as a substitute for ivory

It began as imitation. Ivory, tortoiseshell, coral. The first plastics weren’t meant to replace nature — just to relieve it. Fewer elephants. Same combs.

Then came war. Nylon for parachutes, polyethylene for radar, Bakelite in weapons. The same materials that mimicked elegance were turned toward survival.

Plastic doesn’t decay. It fragments. Not into soil, but into data: microplastic, nanoparticle, traceable but unresolved. A slow unmaking without return.

We now engineer enzymes to degrade it. Worms and fungi, trained to digest what we built to last. Nature, hired to erase our imitation of nature.

Polymer chains hold their shape. Systems do too. Once built, they tend to repeat — regardless of what they were made for.

Plastic was sold as freedom. Now it lines the blood, the soil, the milk. Ubiquity is not invisibility — only fatigue.

It still works. That’s the problem.
