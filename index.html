Magnetic Tape

Once the backbone of data storage, magnetic tape degrades through sticky-shed syndrome, where the binder that holds magnetic particles to the tape base breaks down, causing the tape to shed its memory-holding layer.

Half-life: 10-30 years | Failure mode: Binder degradation

Optical Discs

CDs and DVDs suffer from disc rotâ€”a gradual oxidation of the reflective layer that makes data unreadable. The aluminum layer corrodes, creating dark spots that spread like digital cancer.

Half-life: 20-100 years | Failure mode: Layer oxidation

Flash Memory

NAND flash cells wear out through program/erase cycles. Each write operation traps electrons in the floating gate, gradually degrading the cell's ability to hold charge and distinguish between 0 and 1.

Half-life: 10-100 years | Failure mode: Charge leakage

Hard Drives

Mechanical failure through head crashes, bearing wear, and magnetic domain decay. The read/write heads can physically contact the platter surface, scraping away the magnetic coating that holds data.

Half-life: 3-5 years active | Failure mode: Mechanical wear

Cloud Storage

Distributed across multiple physical media, cloud storage appears permanent but depends on corporate continuity, economic viability, and the maintenance of vast server farms consuming enormous amounts of energy.

Half-life: Unknown | Failure mode: Economic/Corporate

DNA Storage

Biological storage medium with extraordinary density but subject to hydrolysis, oxidation, and enzymatic degradation. Even in ideal conditions, DNA strands break and bases modify over time.

Half-life: 500+ years | Failure mode: Chemical degradation
